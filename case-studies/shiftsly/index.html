Shiftsly: Building a UK SaaS Product from Zero, Founder to Full Stack

The problem

I saw this problem firsthand living in London. Cleaning-team managers run their entire operation, schedules, shift swaps, client sites, through WhatsApp threads. It doesn't scale past a handful of people, none of it is structured data, and no one has visibility into who's covering what. Existing tools like ZenMaid and Jobber either aren't cleaning-specific or are priced and built for teams much larger than the 5-20 person crews that make up the bulk of the market.

Shiftsly is a mobile-first scheduling SaaS built for that gap. UK-native, simpler than the incumbents, priced for small teams.

Architecture

Auth Scheduling core Billing Growth automation

Full product, built and owned solo: Next.js on Vercel, Prisma against a Neon Postgres database, NextAuth for authentication with Google OAuth and magic-link email via Resend (no password-reset tickets), Stripe for subscription billing across 3 live tiers. I designed, built, and shipped every layer myself, from schema to auth flow to billing to UI.

Scheduling core: a weekly schedule table with a mobile carousel view for on-the-go managers. It has to work on a phone screen, since the buyer checks it between job sites, not at a desk. The object model matches the real domain: teams, employees, weekly shifts, and per-employee status.

Billing: three tiers run on Stripe: free up to 5 cleaners, then two paid tiers that scale by team size. I priced around the unit that matters to a cleaning-team manager, headcount, instead of borrowing a generic seat model from a different market.

Growth automation: instead of checking dashboards by hand, I built a Telegram agent that runs twice a day, pulls live signals from Stripe and the product database, and pushes a digest straight to me. It's the same instinct the GTM Engineer role asks for, instrumenting a funnel and shipping automation with whatever tool gets there fastest, applied to my own product instead of someone else's GTM stack.

Validation loop: a dedicated in-product usability-test flow, a multi-step form with NPS scoring reviewed in an internal admin panel, feeds directly into what gets built next. Real users rate real friction points instead of me guessing.

What this demonstrates

Owning the whole system, not a slice of it

Auth, data model, billing, and UI are all one person's decisions. That's the ownership mindset the role asks for: acting as execution and build partner, turning strategy into a production-grade system end to end.

Building the growth instrumentation, not just the product

The Stripe- and database-fed Telegram digest is a self-built signal-to-action pipeline. It matches the enrichment infrastructure and signal-to-action workflows described in the role, scoped to a founder checking metrics instead of a sales team.

Shipping fast, iterating on real feedback

The in-product usability test with NPS scoring closes the loop between what ships and what confuses users. Prototype, measure, adjust, repeat.

Picking the right stack independently

I made every technical decision solo under real constraints of cost, support burden, and time to ship: Neon over a self-hosted database, NextAuth over a paid auth vendor, magic-link over password auth. No team handed these down.
